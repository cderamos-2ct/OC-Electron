## TODD DELANO — Playing Devil's Advocate

**Todd's mindset:** "We're burning $11,200/month on a dev team and I'm hearing about problems, not profits. Christian wants more resources, more authority, and a bigger seat at the table. I need to see the math that justifies any of that before I sign off on anything."

Clarity goes to beta this Friday, February 28, with go-live March 7-10. That opens the BPO vertical, which is our biggest revenue category. The team isn't just building — they're building assets we monetize repeatedly. I can put together a project-by-project revenue attribution by end of week if that would help frame the picture."

**What NOT to say:**
Don't say "we're investing in the future" without tying it to a specific dollar figure or timeline. Todd hears that as "I don't have numbers."

### Question 2: "Why should I give you two more technicians when you can't fully utilize the 9.3 you have?"

> "You're telling me you need two more low-wage technicians. But you also just said we have 9 active projects across 9.3 FTE and the math doesn't work. So why would I throw more people at a resource allocation problem? How do I know you won't come back in three months asking for two more?"

**Christian's best response:**

Don't frame it as "the team is overwhelmed" — Todd will hear that as a management problem, not a resource problem.

**What NOT to say:**
Don't promise "we won't miss it" — Todd respects honest risk assessment more than bravado.

### Question 4: "You're asking for a bigger role. What exactly does that look like, and what does it cost me?"

---

**Pressure Points to Watch:** Todd gets frustrated when conversations stay abstract. The moment you lose specificity, you lose him. He also doesn't like hearing about problems without solutions attached.

**How to win Todd over:** Lead with the POP revenue number. Show the math on the technicians ($1,500-2,000/month cost vs. developer throughput gain). Frame every ask as "this is what it costs, this is what it returns." Todd will nod when the ratio makes sense.

## THE HARDEST MOMENT IN THE ROOM

### The question that could derail the meeting:

> **Todd:** "Christian, let me be direct. You're asking for governance authority, more resources, a strategic pivot, and platform ownership. Kyle brings in the business. Eric has the platform vision. Craig runs operations. Mark manages the money. What exactly is everyone else doing if you get everything you're asking for? Are you trying to run the company?"

### Why this is dangerous:
This reframes every ask as a power grab. It puts Kyle, Eric, Craig, and Mark on the defensive because it implies Christian is encroaching on their domains. Even if Todd doesn't mean it that way, it forces everyone in the room to evaluate whether Christian's asks threaten their role.

### How Christian should handle it:

**Take a breath. Do not get defensive. Do not laugh it off.**

"Todd, I appreciate the directness and I want to be equally direct. I'm not asking to run the company. I'm asking to run the technology delivery — which is the job you hired me for. Every ask on my list today is about removing obstacles to delivery. Governance means project asks get a capacity check before they become commitments — that helps Kyle sell with confidence, it helps Craig plan operations, and it helps Mark forecast costs. The technicians free up developer time. The SSOT sprint builds the platform Eric has been championing. The VG formalization protects John legally.

"I'm not trying to own anyone's lane. I'm trying to make sure the tech lane delivers on what everyone in this room needs from it. If anything on my list steps on someone's domain, I want to hear that right now so we can adjust.
